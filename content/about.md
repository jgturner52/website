When I was shopping my dissertation-turned-first-book to publishers, an acquisitions editor asked me, "Are you one of them?" As in, was I "one of" the evangelicals about whom I was writing? He may have simply wanted to better understand the project and my angle on it. Regardless of his intent, the question reflects a suspicion that pervades the academy, namely, that it's problematic for scholars to write about their own religious traditions. Whether or not suspicion is merited, I agree with those who argue that historians and other scholars should acknowledge their backgrounds and be transparent about any biases. In the meantime, complete neutrality is impossible.

I am a cradle-to-halfway-to-the-grave Presbyterian who has lived his life on the boundary between mainline and evangelical Protestantism. I belong to Burke Presbyterian Church, a relatively large congregation in the suburbs of northern Virginia. I grew up attending Young Life and InterVarsity Christian Fellowship. Those experiences partly prompted me to study U.S. History, because I wanted to understand the institutions that shaped my own religious culture.

My dissertation became my first book, on Bill Bright and Campus Crusade for Christ, which won a Christianity Today book award in 2009. I sometimes explain that I'm a Presbyterian who became interested in parachurch organizations, an evangelical who voted against Donald Trump (both times), and a churchgoing Christian who set out to study Mormonism. (I obviously don't fit neatly into any of these categories.)

After the Campus Crusade book, I explored terrain less familiar to me. I journeyed to the Lane in South Royalton, Vermont, site of the Joseph Smith Birthplace and made my first visit to Salt Lake City in 2010. My research led to *Brigham Young: Pioneer Prophet* and then *The Mormon Jesus: A Biography*, as I became increasingly interested in how Mormonism fit within the broader landscape of American Christianity.

After two books on Mormon history, I turned back to a topic more familiar for a historian of American Protestantism. *They Knew They Were Pilgrims* examined the Pilgrims and Plymouth Colony.

In my most recent book, *Joseph Smith: The Rise and Fall of an American Prophet*, I returned to the subject of Latter-day Saint history with a biography of Mormonism's founder.

I think a mix of insider and outsider perspectives enriches our understanding of religious groups. I always welcome feedback on whether my work is accurate and fair.
